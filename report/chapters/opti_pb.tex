\section{Optimization problem}\label{sec:opti_pb}

\subsection{Linear decomposition of the solution}

Let $T(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathbf{x})$ be the solution of the problem.

First, we define $T_0(\mathbf{x})$ as the solution to the physical problem without any internal heat sources ($f = 0$), but subjected to the exact same external boundary conditions as $T$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:T0}
    \begin{cases}
        -\mathrm{div}(k\nabla T_0) = 0 & \text{in } \Omega, \\
        T_0 = T_c & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{top}}, \\
        \partial_n T_0 + h T_0 = h T_e & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{bottom}}, \\
        \partial_n T_0 = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{side}}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Next, we introduce the residual temperature field $U(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathbf{x}) = T(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathbf{x}) - T_0(\mathbf{x})$. By subtracting the system of equations for $T_0$ from the system for $T$, and by linearity of the differential operators, $U$ satisfies:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:global_U}
    \begin{cases}
        -\mathrm{div}(k\nabla U) = \sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i \mathbb{1}_{C_i} & \text{in } \Omega, \\
        U = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{top}}, \\
        \partial_n U + h U = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{bottom}}, \\
        \partial_n U = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{side}}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Since the partial differential operator $-\mathrm{div}(k\nabla \cdot)$ along with these homogeneous boundary conditions forms a purely linear operator, the solution $U$ depends linearly on the source term $\sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i \mathbb{1}_{C_i}$.

By the superposition principle, we can therefore define $T_i(\mathbf{x})$ for each $i \in \{1, \dots, 6\}$ as the elementary solution of~\cref{eq:global_U}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:Ti}
    \begin{cases}
        -\mathrm{div}(k\nabla T_i) = \mathbb{1}_{C_i} & \text{in } \Omega, \\
        T_i = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{top}}, \\
        \partial_n T_i + h T_i = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{bottom}}, \\
        \partial_n T_i = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_{\text{side}}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Due to linearity, it immediately follows that $U(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i T_i(\mathbf{x})$. Substituting this back into $T = T_0 + U$, we obtain the decomposition of the temperature field:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:decomp}
    T(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \mathbf{x}) = T_0(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i \, T_i(\mathbf{x}).
\end{equation}
where $T_0$ follows~\cref{eq:T0} and each $T_i$ follows~\cref{eq:Ti}.

\subsection{Reduction to a linear system}\label{sec:linear_opti_system}

We seek the optimal vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^* = {(\alpha_1^*, \dots, \alpha_6^*)}^T$ that minimizes the cost function:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cost_func}
    J(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{1}{2}\int_S |T(\boldsymbol{\alpha},\mathbf{x}) - T_s|^2 \dd \mathbf{x}.
\end{equation}

By substituting the linear decomposition from \cref{eq:decomp}, $J$ becomes a convex quadratic function of $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$:
\begin{equation}
    J(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \frac{1}{2}\int_S {\left( T_0(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i T_i(\mathbf{x}) - T_s \right)}^2 \dd \mathbf{x}.
\end{equation}

The necessary and sufficient first-order optimality conditions for this unconstrained convex minimization problem are $\frac{\partial J}{\partial \alpha_j}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*) = 0$ for all $j \in \{1,\dots,6\}$. Differentiating $J$ gives:
\begin{equation}
    \int_S \left( T_0(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_{i=1}^6 \alpha_i^* T_i(\mathbf{x}) - T_s \right) T_j(\mathbf{x}) \dd \mathbf{x} = 0, \quad \forall j \in \{1,\dots,6\}.
\end{equation}

By separating the terms that depend on $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$ from the known quantities, we obtain the following $6 \times 6$ linear system:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:syst_opt}
    \boxed{M\,\boldsymbol{\alpha}^* = \mathbf{b}, \qquad \text{with } M_{ij} = \int_S T_i\,T_j \dd \mathbf{x}, \quad \text{and } b_j = \int_S (T_s - T_0)\,T_j \dd \mathbf{x}.}
\end{equation}


$M$ is symmetric by construction. Furthermore, for a vector $\mathbf{v} = {(v_1, \dots, v_6)}^T$, we have:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}^T M \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^6 \sum_{j=1}^6 v_i \left( \int_S T_i(\mathbf{x}) T_j(\mathbf{x}) \dd \mathbf{x} \right) v_j = \int_S {\left( \sum_{i=1}^6 v_i T_i(\mathbf{x}) \right)}^2 \dd \mathbf{x} \ge 0,
\end{equation}
which proves that $M$ is positive semi-definite. Assuming the localized thermal footprints ${(T_i|_S)}_{i=1}^6$ are linearly independent over $S$ (which is the case here), the integral vanishes if and only if $\mathbf{v} = 0$. Therefore, $M$ is positive definite, which guarantees the invertibility of the linear system~\cref{eq:syst_opt} and the uniqueness of the optimal control $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$.

\subsection{Optimal coefficients and comments}

The resolution of the linear system~\cref{eq:syst_opt} with $T_s = 400\,\mathrm{K}$ yields the optimal control vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$, which dictates the power intensity of each individual resistor. The computed optimal values are:
\begin{align*}
    \alpha_1^* &= 159\,601, & \alpha_2^* &= 168\,331, & \alpha_3^* &= 158\,176, \\
    \alpha_4^* &= 502\,764, & \alpha_5^* &= 412\,308, & \alpha_6^* &= 497\,148.
\end{align*}
The resulting optimized temperature distribution is displayed in \cref{fig:optimalPb}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig/optimalPb.eps}
    \caption{Temperature distribution inside the oven computed with the optimal heat sources $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$.}\label{fig:optimalPb}
\end{figure}

The objective of the optimization is successfully met, as the residual $L^2$-error over the cooking region $S$ drops to a low value of $3.06$. Visually, the temperature field within $S$ (the central rectangle) is almost uniform.

We can also remark that the optimization algorithm naturally accounted for the asymmetric boundary conditions of the oven. The top resistors ($\alpha_4^*, \alpha_5^*, \alpha_6^*$) must operate at a significantly higher intensity ($\approx 5 \times 10^5$) compared to the bottom resistors ($\alpha_1^*, \alpha_2^*, \alpha_3^* \approx 1.6 \times 10^5$). This perfectly balances the distinct heat dissipation rates of the upper and lower walls (Dirichlet condition is more dissipative than Robin condition).

Furthermore, the resistors located closer to the center of $S$ ($\alpha_2^*$ and $\alpha_5^*$) have different values than the ones located on the edges (one is lower and one is higher).

\subsection{Design of an improved oven configuration}

While the previous optimization yielded a satisfactory $L^2$-error, we can have better results. By modifying the location, shape, and number of the heat sources, we propose a new configuration aimed at achieving a more naturally uniform heat distribution.

So, we chose to increase the number of resistors from 6 to 8. We distribute them symmetrically around the cooking zone: two on the top, two on the bottom, two on the left, and two on the right.

Moreover, the circular point sources are replaced by rectangular resistors, in order to follow the shape of the cooking region.

We implemented this new configuration in FreeFem++ and solved the optimization problem using the same approach as \cref{sec:linear_opti_system}.

As shown in \cref{fig:improvedOven}, the new optimal temperature distribution is remarkably homogeneous. 
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig/improvedOven.eps}
    \caption{Temperature distribution for the proposed 8-strip configuration.}\label{fig:improvedOven}
\end{figure}

The residual $L^2$-error drops significantly from $3.06$ to $2.167$. Furthermore, the optimal power coefficients $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^*$ require lower peak intensities, proving that this design is also far more energy-efficient and realistic for industrial manufacturing.
